 Abstract

 

This case examines the challenges of reporting on a private individual’s sexual preference. In March 2008, the Washington Post received a tip that Army Major Alan Rogers was the first avowedly gay military officer to be killed in Iraq. If true, the life story of the much-decorated and admired Major Rogers would go far to disprove the view—held by opponents of gays in the military—that gay officers and enlisted personnel undermined morale. But as reporter Donna St. George began her reporting, she discovered a murkier reality—that while Rogers was indisputably gay, it was far from clear that he was open about it, especially to his military colleagues. While Post policy forbade “outing” a living gay person without his/her consent, Rogers was dead, and had no immediate family. He had expressed no wishes on this matter. But his story could have important policy implications. Further, to report on Rogers’ funeral without mentioning his sexual preference could be considered deception by omission and could come back to haunt the Post. But to identify him as gay could violate the careful compromise by which Rogers had apparently lived his life. Post Executive Editor Leonard Downie, Jr., had to make a decision.

 

Classroom discussion can focus on the tension between the historical record and the individual. When does one trump the other? Should a news organization embrace an opportunity to change public perception or influence public policy? What are the implications for a newspaper if it knows something to be true and does not publish it? Is that censorship, or respect for the individual? Students should come to appreciate the nuances that accompany the treatment of gender issues in news stories. They could also consider how useful editorial policy guidelines really are.

 

The case can be used in a course about reporting on gender, ethnicity or race; about editorial decisionmaking; or about ethics in journalism.
